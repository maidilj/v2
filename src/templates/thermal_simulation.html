Project 02 — Computational Modelling

Heat-Channel Embedded
Smart Materials: FE Simulation

Finite element thermal simulation of heat-channel networks embedded within 3D-printed thermoplastic composites — developed at HP-NTU Corporate Lab to enable targeted, stimulus-responsive deformation in wearable biomedical devices. Contributed to a filed US patent (US20240109246A1).

MATLAB PDE Toolbox FEM Thermal Modelling Composite Materials Patent Filed

Problem Statement

Wearable biomedical devices such as face masks, elbow patches, and bracelets require materials that can deform precisely on-demand — conforming to the human body at specific joints without bulk heating. Conventional shape-memory polymers apply heat uniformly, which is slow, energy-inefficient, and imprecise.

The objective was to design and simulate a heat-channel network embedded within Multi-Jet Fusion (MJF) 3D-printed polyamide composites that could conduct heat selectively to target deformable joint regions — raising them past their glass transition temperature (Tg ≈ 50–60°C) while keeping the surrounding casing safe for skin contact.

Fig. 1 — Potential applications of heat-responsive materials for wearable devices, fabricated prototypes and the underlying mechanism utilizing embedded heat channels additively manufuctured with HP's Multi-Jet Fusion (MJF) 3D printing technology.

Modelling Approach

Geometry & Mesh: A 2D cross-sectional geometry was constructed representing the composite assembly — comprising the heat channel, joint region sized at approximately 40mm × 10mm. The mesh was built using triangular elements in MATLAB's PDE Toolbox, with refinement concentrated at the material interfaces where heat flux gradients are steepest.

Fig. 2 — Triangular FE mesh of the composite cross-section. Red boundaries demarcate material interfaces between heat channel, joint region, and polyamide casing. Mesh nodes: 2,271 · Triangles: 4,336.

Material Definition: Carbon black fusing agent (FA) concentration was the primary variable, tested at 10 wt%, 20 wt%, and 30 wt%. Increasing FA content raises both thermal conductivity and infrared absorptivity of the composite. Boundary conditions applied a 160°C heat source at the channel inlet and a convective coefficient of h = 5 W/m²K at exposed surfaces, simulating still-air conditions on the skin-facing side.

Two Heat Transfer Modes Investigated: The study compared (1) conductive heat transfer through the carbon-doped polyamide matrix, and (2) Joule heating via embedded silver nanowire/nanoparticle networks acting as resistive elements. Joule heating was modelled by varying conductor cross-sectional area and path length to characterise the power-resistance tradeoff.

Results

The thermal distribution map below shows the steady-state temperature field for the varying (10-30 wt%) carbon composite under conductive heating. The heat front propagates from the left-side source and attenuates sharply at the air channel boundary — confirming the insulation design effectively limits heat leakage to the casing surface.

Fig. 3 — Steady-state thermal distribution (T, °C). Red = high temperature zone at heat source; blue = ambient. Heat effectively propogates into the deformable region in the 20 wt% and 30 wt% designs.

The transient response curves below show joint temperature over time across FA concentrations. At 30 wt% FA, the joint region exceeds Tg (~50°C) within approximately 3 minutes — fast enough for practical actuation. A key finding was that casing temperature rise can outpace joint temperature rise at lower FA concentrations, identifying thermal leakage as the critical failure mode to engineer against.

Fig. 4 — Joint region temperature vs. time for 10, 20, and 30 wt% fusing agent. Dashed lines mark the glass transition temperature (~50°C) and the time window for actuation. 30 wt% achieves Tg crossover in ~3 minutes.

Key Findings

Higher carbon concentration directly accelerates Tg attainment but also increases thermal leakage risk to the skin-contact surface. This identified FA concentration as a dual-constraint design variable — requiring optimisation against both actuation speed and surface safety simultaneously.

This finding provided evidence for the need to switch to an alternative mode of conduction such as electric conduction to achieve the desired actuation speed while ensuring safe surface temperature for usage.

These simulation-driven findings directly shaped the composite material specifications that were incorporated into the patent filing.

Relevance to Forensic Fire Science

The analytical framework applied here — modelling heat transfer through heterogeneous material systems, identifying failure modes at thermal boundaries, and tracing temperature propagation to its physical cause — is methodologically identical to the process used in fire origin and cause investigation. Whether examining how heat channels through a composite wearable or how thermal energy propagates through a building compartment following ignition, the governing physics are the same: Fourier's law, boundary conditions, material thermal properties, and the critical thresholds at which materials change state. This simulation work built a rigorous, first-principles intuition for thermal behaviour under controlled and uncontrolled conditions — directly applicable to fire forensics.
